Everyday is filled with wonder as I 'wonder' who will 'wander' into the garden today

These Kookaburras usually travel in a pack of 3. These 2 are male and female with the third an older bird, possibly a parent. One can see all 3 fly into the back yard just as dawn rises. The female is a beautiful bird who chats with me with "clook clook" sounds. They have been in the area for decades and have visited my yard from before I arrived. The female who I have nicknamed simply "Kooka" comes during her breeding season between November and Februaury each year. She will spend hours atop the hills hoist staring into the garden and then swoop in and pick up a lizard or insect. Other birds fear her and make distress calls, some who have nearby nests try to chase her away, other birds simply hide. She has a direct line of sight to me inside the kitchen, so when she wants a piece of meat she will stare straight at me. If I don't come out she will call out with the trademark Kookaburra laugh.

This is the older female superb fairy wren who has been nicknamed Jill. A dainty and delicate bird who is pictured with a twig in her beak for her nest. She and her male mate who is nicknamed Neville have lived here for several years. In 2013, they built a nest in a pot plant and laid an egg. Unfortunately a predator destroyed the nest and egg. This was very sad and I could not see or hear the wrens for weeks. Then one day they re-appeared and made a new nest in the macadamia tree. A few weeks later a female wren appeared with her parents, Neville and Jill. She has been nicknamed Jill junior. In 2014, another baby wren was born, another female. So, now the Wren family consists of 3 females and 1 male. They're trill can be heard as they flit around the yard.

A couple of blackbirds built their nest inside a stone carved wall mounted pot. This pot hung outside the laundry under a roof. Each day the male and female blackbirds took turns to sit on their eggs. Over a few weeks I saw 6 hatchlings turn into birds, who would soon fly the coop. Blackbirds are known for building nests close to people as a way of protecting themselves from predators. They are also renowned for their birdsong which is why the English convicts brought them to Australia in the 1800's so they could hear the enchanting birdsong of the blackbirds.
